Moment
a hoop house for Phipps Conservatory and Botanical Gardens


CMU School of Architecture

Collaborators: Christoph Eckrich, Cathy Dong, Gil Jang, Angelina Shi, Owen Haft



a hoop house

Our design is driven by the demands of accessibility, with careful consideration of the specific site conditions that influence growth. The primary purpose is to extend the growing season of the plants inside, but how do we best do this? One factor is, of course, the passive regulation of the environment, but perhaps the more pivotal factor in terms of the plant’s well-being is its maintenance and care. Having a dedicated caretaker for our plants is invaluable to the plant’s growth.

This moment of gardener/hoop-house/plant interaction is the most important for the life of our plants, we find it key to make it the most beautiful aspect of the project. Whether that be through an innovative mechanism, an interesting hinge structure, or a striking opening movement, this moment will be the center of our design.

Of course we also cannot ignore the prevailing environmental factors. We must be cognizant of the path of the sun, creating a design that takes advantage of the already excellent exposure on the site. We must understand the varying climates that our design will be utilized in, creating methods for adaptation to the external temperature and conditions. Our forms will follow the constraints of the steel conduit and the effect of heat shrink plastic. MOMENT is the expression of an event that takes place between garden and gardener. This juncture acts not only as protection but also as presentation.

Not a tedium, but an occasion

The site of the installation is Phipps Conservatory and Botanical Gardens in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania. Phipps had graciously allowed this studio to install its works in the “Edible Garden” and had also offered to take care of the plants that would be housed in the projects. Because of this, our design focused on the artistic presentation and interactive design of the house rather than functional aspects such as natural watering, however, the opening of the design includes two modes, one half-opened which highlights watering, and one fully opened which allows for full access harvest.

The form of the hoop house imitates nature through its square and golden rectangle geometry. Not only does the form highlight nature, but the brackets of the two end modules follow the sun path. The form both increases the surface area facing the sun for increased input of natural sunlight, and also allows the modules, with its shrunk plastic, to fold inside one another.

This design highlights the opening and closing aspect of hoop houses. The primary opening action, or the full opening, is to be used for presentations and harvesting. The secondary opening action, or half-opening, is to be used for watering and general ventilation. These two opening types were created due to the harsh winter climate of Pittsburgh and also because a full opening every time the plants are watered would cause too much heat loss.

Finally, Moment highlights the use of steel conduit and handmade joinery. In order to utilize these materials to their fullest capacity, unique joints were studied and detailed out. The main joint in this system is a hinge system which allows the modules to fold into each other. The materials were purchased and modified by our group to make these effective joints.
